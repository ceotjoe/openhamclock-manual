Ham Radio Glossary

Quick definitions for terms used throughout this manual.

TermDefinition
APRSAutomatic Packet Reporting System. A digital protocol used to transmit position, weather, and text data over radio.
AOSAcquisition of Signal — the moment a satellite rises above your horizon and becomes workable.
AzimuthCompass bearing in degrees (0° = North, 90° = East, 180° = South, 270° = West). Used for antenna pointing.
BandA range of radio frequencies allocated by the ITU for amateur use. e.g. "20 m band" = 14.0–14.35 MHz.
CallsignA unique identifier assigned to a licensed amateur radio station by its national authority (e.g. W1AW, G4ABC, JA1ZZZ).
CQA general call inviting any station to respond. "CQ DX" specifically invites distant stations.
CWContinuous Wave — Morse code. Still widely used for DX and contesting due to its efficiency.
Doppler shiftThe change in received frequency caused by relative motion between transmitter and receiver. Significant for satellite contacts.
DXDistant station. In HF operating, typically means a station in another country or rare entity.
DXCCDX Century Club — the ARRL award for working 100+ countries. Also used as shorthand for "country" in the context of rare DX entities.
EMEEarth-Moon-Earth (moonbounce). Contacts made by bouncing signals off the moon's surface.
FT8A weak-signal digital mode (Franke-Taylor design, 8-FSK) with 15-second periods. The most widely used HF mode worldwide as of 2024.
Great-circle pathThe shortest distance between two points on a sphere — the actual path radio waves travel around the Earth.
Grid squareA Maidenhead Locator System cell used to describe geographic position. 4-character (e.g. IO91) or 6-character (IO91wm) resolution.
Grey lineThe twilight boundary between day and night. A zone of enhanced HF propagation, especially on 80 m and 40 m.
HFHigh Frequency — 3–30 MHz. The primary amateur radio spectrum for worldwide contacts via ionospheric propagation.
IonosphereLayers of the upper atmosphere (60–600 km) ionised by solar radiation, enabling HF radio signals to travel around the Earth.
K-index / KpA measure of geomagnetic disturbance (0–9). High values indicate solar storm activity that degrades HF propagation.
LEOLow Earth Orbit — typically 200–2000 km altitude. Most amateur satellites are in LEO, with pass windows of 5–15 minutes.
LOSLoss of Signal — the moment a satellite sets below your horizon.
Maidenhead locatorSee Grid square.
POTAParks on the Air — an amateur radio programme where operators activate national parks and similar areas.
PropagationHow radio waves travel from transmitter to receiver. HF propagation relies on reflection from the ionosphere.
QSOA two-way radio contact between two stations.
RBNReverse Beacon Network — a global network of automated CW/RTTY receivers that spot CQ calls automatically.
SFISolar Flux Index (F10.7) — a measure of solar radio emission and proxy for ionospheric conditions. Higher = better HF propagation.
SGP4Simplified General Perturbations 4 — the standard orbital propagation model used to predict satellite positions from TLE data.
SOTASummits on the Air — an amateur radio programme where operators activate qualifying mountain summits.
SSNSunspot Number (Wolf Number) — a daily count of visible sunspot groups. Correlates with SFI and HF propagation quality.
TLETwo-Line Elements — a standardised format for describing a satellite's orbital parameters, used by SGP4 to predict positions.
VHF/UHFVery High Frequency (30–300 MHz) and Ultra High Frequency (300 MHz–3 GHz). Most amateur satellites use these bands.
WSPRWeak Signal Propagation Reporter — a beacon mode used to map HF propagation by reporting who hears whom.
WWFFWorld Wide Flora & Fauna — an amateur radio programme activating nature reserves and protected areas.

Contributing

OpenHamClock is open-source (MIT licence) and welcomes contributions. Whether you want to fix a bug, add a feature, improve a translation, or just report a problem:

  • Bug reports & feature requests — GitHub Issues
  • Code contributions — fork the repo, work on a branch, open a pull request against main. See CONTRIBUTING.md for guidelines.
  • Translations — translation files live in src/lang/. 16 languages are currently at 100% coverage. New languages are very welcome.
  • Documentation — this manual lives at github.com/accius/openhamclock-manual. Pull requests for corrections and improvements are appreciated.
